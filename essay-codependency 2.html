Codependency · Essay No. 01

Why Do Women
Feel Codependent?

Nisreen Burhan 8 min read April 2026

This is not a clinical paper. This is a letter — to you, to the version of yourself that has been giving and giving and wondering why it never feels like enough.

What is codependency — really?

For someone hearing this word for the first time — codependency means deriving all of your emotions — positive or negative — your sense of personal worth, your right to joy or to grief, from another person. Not from yourself. From them.

Many women may not have felt this consciously. But perhaps there are small signs, quiet signals, that whoever is reading this right now can begin to notice in themselves.

—

The rush toward someone else's life

Every young woman has been through relationships and dating. And sometimes we find ourselves drawn to someone who carries the qualities we’ve always dreamed of. But why?

Yes, we deserve someone who shares our values. But often — driven by a desire to feel special, to hold a certain place in the world — we rush toward the most handsome, the most successful, the most generous. Someone who seems to carry everything we ever wanted, offered to us without us having to reach for it ourselves.

And if we look a little deeper, we find something rooted there: a distrust of our own capabilities. A quiet, persistent belief that we are not enough on our own. And that distrust is what drove us — sometimes desperately — to place our dreams, our sense of home, our vision of a full life, into the hands of someone else.

This is the centre of the problem. I am not saying a woman shouldn’t build, work, and reach for everything she wants — she absolutely should. But there are social conditions, traditions, and long-held beliefs that have restricted that possibility for women for generations. And so the inner feeling of unworthiness found something to lean on. A reason to stay still. A permission to not move toward herself.

This is one of the most common patterns — and we can see it in ourselves and in the women closest to us.

—

Where did this begin? The first years.

It began, most often, in the first years of life.

A child does not develop a genuine sense of self until after the first five years. During that time, the mother and the father are a direct mirror — the child sees themselves reflected in how they are treated, spoken to, held or ignored. The child has no power of their own. They depend entirely on their parents for food, warmth, safety, closeness, and the feeling of being seen.

So they learn, very early, to read the room. To speak when the parent is in a good mood. To feel worthy only when a specific kind of praise arrives. To shrink when the environment becomes unsafe.

When a child grows up with a parent who is inconsistent, volatile, absent, or cruel — the nervous system adapts. It learns that love is not reliable. That safety is conditional. That the way to survive is to monitor the other person’s emotions, not your own. The child stops building a sense of value from within — because there was never a stable enough mirror to reflect one back.

And they carry that adaptation into adulthood. Into relationships. Into the way they love.

—

Why is this wound so much wider in women?

Because women are raised in environments that prescribe a very specific image: be obedient. Listen to men — they know better. Care for your parents no matter how they treat you. Don’t dream too large — you’ll be a wife and a mother in the end. Be beautiful and appealing so that men will want you.

Almost everything in the environment tells a woman to perform for someone else. To exist in relation to others. To measure her worth by how well she is received.

The first wound: she lost her sense of self. The second: a society that built a cage around that emptiness and decorated it with expectations.

And so women continue living lives that are completely at odds with what moves inside them — their real desires, their real capabilities — if those were ever given room to exist at all.

They place their unlived dreams onto one person. Their need for love, for safety, for a different life — all of it, onto one man. And when that man leaves, they fall into a grief with no visible bottom. A depression unlike anything else they’ve known.

Because he was the mirror. And without him, there is nothing left to reflect her back to herself. She cannot move forward — not because she is weak, but because no one ever taught her how to be a person on her own terms.

—

Signs that a woman may be living in codependency

These are not judgments. They are recognitions. Read them slowly.

01

She is afraid to express her own desires — afraid of losing her place in someone’s eyes if she does.

02

She feels empty without a significant person in her life — a man, a child, someone — who gives her the feeling that her life has meaning and value.

03

A recurring inner voice that says: I can’t. I’m not ready. I’ll do it later. Always more time. Always later.

04

Her mood rises and falls entirely based on the mood of whoever she depends on. If they are upset, her world becomes unsafe. If they are calm, she can breathe.

05

She apologises constantly. She over-explains constantly. For things that are not her fault. For simply having needs.

06

She confuses love with fear. She believes she loves someone deeply — and underneath that love is a terror of being left alone with herself.

So if you found yourself somewhere in what you just read — in one of these patterns, or in the story of how they form — that recognition is real. And it matters.

But awareness alone is not enough. Knowing the name of something does not heal it. Understanding where a wound came from does not close it.

That is a different kind of work. And you don’t have to do it alone.

If any of this felt familiar — I’m here.
